SECTION 1: THE 2006 IAU RESOLUTION AND THE PLANET DEFINITION DEBATE
---
On August 24, 2006, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) passed Resolution B5 during its 26th General Assembly in Prague, Czech Republic. This historic resolution formally established a three-part definition for a "planet" in the Solar System and created a new category of celestial objects called "dwarf planets."

According to Resolution B5, a celestial body must satisfy three strict criteria to be classified as a planet:
1. Orbit the Sun: It must be in orbit around the Sun (and not be a satellite/moon of another body).
2. Hydrostatic Equilibrium: It must have sufficient mass for its self-gravity to overcome rigid body forces, allowing it to assume a nearly round (spherical) shape.
3. Clearing the Neighborhood: It must have cleared the neighborhood around its orbit, meaning it is gravitationally dominant in its orbital path and has either consumed or swept away other bodies of comparable size.

A "dwarf planet" is defined under the same resolution as a celestial body that:
1. Orbits the Sun.
2. Has sufficient mass to assume hydrostatic equilibrium (nearly round).
3. Has NOT cleared the neighborhood around its orbit.
4. Is not a satellite (moon) of another celestial body.

All other objects orbiting the Sun, excluding satellites, are collectively categorized as "Small Solar System Bodies" (SSSBs). This category includes asteroids, comets, and trans-Neptunian objects (TNOs).

SECTION 2: PROMINENT DWARF PLANETS AND THEIR DETAILED CHARACTERISTICS
---
This section outlines the five dwarf planets officially recognized by the IAU, along with key physical parameters, discovery histories, and satellite systems.

1. PLUTO (Kuiper Belt)
   - Discovery Year: 1930 (Announced on March 13, 1930)
   - Discovered By: Clyde Tombaugh at the Lowell Observatory in Flagstaff, Arizona.
   - Physical Profile: Diameter is approximately 2,376 kilometers. The surface is composed of 98% nitrogen ice, with trace amounts of methane and carbon monoxide ices covering a dense rocky core.
   - Orbit Period: 248 Earth years, with a highly eccentric and inclined orbit that periodically brings it closer to the Sun than Neptune.
   - Temperature: Ranges from -230 degrees C to -220 degrees C.
   - Moons: Pluto has five known natural satellites: Charon (the largest, discovered in 1978 by James Christy), Nix, Hydra (both discovered in 2005), Kerberos (discovered in 2011), and Styx (discovered in 2012).

2. ERIS (Scattered Disc)
   - Discovery Year: 2005 (Announced in July 2005 by Mike Brown, Chad Trujillo, and David Rabinowitz)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter is approximately 2,326 kilometers. Eris is about 27% more massive than Pluto, despite having a slightly smaller volume. Its surface is covered in a highly reflective methane ice layer.
   - Orbit Period: 557 Earth years.
   - Moons: Eris has one known moon named Dysnomia.
   - Significance: The discovery of Eris was the primary catalyst for the 2006 planet definition debate, as it was initially thought to be larger than Pluto.

3. CERES (Asteroid Belt)
   - Discovery Year: 1801 (January 1, by Giuseppe Piazzi at the Palermo Observatory in Sicily)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter is approximately 940 kilometers. Ceres is the largest object in the asteroid belt and accounts for approximately 33% (one-third) of the belt's total mass.
   - Orbit Period: 4.6 Earth years.
   - Moons: Ceres has zero known natural satellites (0 moons).
   - Features: Discovered to contain bright reflective spots inside Occator Crater, which are deposits of sodium carbonate and ammonium chloride left behind by evaporating briny water.

4. HAUMEA (Kuiper Belt)
   - Discovery Year: 2004 (Announced by Mike Brown's team at Caltech, with a controversial independent claim by Jose Luis Ortiz Moreno's team in Spain)
   - Physical Profile: Dimensions are approximately 2,322 x 1,704 x 1,138 kilometers, representing a highly elongated triaxial ellipsoid (resembling an American football). This shape is caused by its rapid rotation, completing one full rotation in just 3.9 hours.
   - Orbit Period: 284 Earth years.
   - Moons: Haumea has two known moons: Hi'iaka and Namaka.
   - Ring System: In January 2017, scientists discovered that Haumea possesses a ring system, making it the first trans-Neptunian object confirmed to have one.

5. MAKEMAKE (Kuiper Belt)
   - Discovery Year: 2005 (March 31, by Mike Brown's team)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter is approximately 1,430 kilometers. The surface is extremely cold (around -243 degrees C) and is covered in methane, ethane, and nitrogen ices.
   - Orbit Period: 309 Earth years.
   - Moons: Makemake has one known moon named S/2015 (136472) 1 (nicknamed MK2), discovered in 2015 using the Hubble Space Telescope.

SECTION 3: ASTROBIOLOGY OF SUBSURFACE OCEANS ON ICY MOONS
---
Beyond dwarf planets, planetary astrobiology focuses heavily on the icy moons of the outer Solar System. These bodies contain vast subsurface liquid water oceans maintained by tidal heating rather than solar radiation.

1. EUROPA (Moon of Jupiter)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter of 3,121 kilometers. It possesses a global liquid water ocean beneath a 15-to-25 kilometer thick ice shell.
   - Ocean Depth: The subsurface ocean is estimated to be 60 to 150 kilometers deep, containing more liquid water than all of Earth's oceans combined.
   - Tidal Heating: Driven by a 4:2:1 Laplace orbital resonance with Io and Ganymede. The gravitational tugging from these neighboring moons deforms Europa, creating internal friction and heat.
   - Surface Age: Low impact crater density indicates a young, active surface estimated to be between 20 and 180 million years old.

2. ENCELADUS (Moon of Saturn)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter of only 504 kilometers. Despite its small size, it is geologically active.
   - Plumes: The Cassini spacecraft discovered massive plumes of water vapor, ice grains, salts, and organic molecules erupting from fractures in the south polar region.
   - Tiger Stripes: These eruption fractures are named Sulci (Damascus, Baghdad, Alexandria, and Cairo Sulci).
   - Chemistry: Cassini detected molecular hydrogen (H2) and silica nanoparticles in the plumes, indicating active hydrothermal vents at the ocean floor where water reacts with a hot rocky core (serpentinization).

3. TITAN (Moon of Saturn)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter of 5,150 kilometers (larger than Mercury). It has a dense atmosphere (1.45 atmospheres surface pressure) composed of 95% nitrogen and 5% methane.
   - Liquids: Titan has stable liquid hydrocarbon lakes and seas on its surface (composed of methane and ethane), such as Kraken Mare.
   - Subsurface Ocean: Gravity data indicates a deep, highly saline liquid water-ammonia ocean below its icy crust.

SECTION 4: EXPLORATION MISSION ARCHIVES
---
Five robotic space missions have provided the foundation for our modern understanding of these remote worlds:

1. GALILEO MISSION (NASA)
   - Operation: 1989 (Launch) to 2003 (Impacted Jupiter).
   - Key Achievement: Confirmed the presence of an induced magnetic field around Europa, proving the existence of a salty subsurface ocean.

2. CASSINI-HUYGENS MISSION (NASA/ESA)
   - Operation: 1997 (Launch) to 2017 (Impacted Saturn).
   - Key Achievement: Discovered Enceladus' active plumes and mapped Titan's surface lakes, landing the Huygens probe on Titan on January 14, 2005.

3. DAWN MISSION (NASA)
   - Operation: 2007 (Launch) to 2018 (End of mission at Ceres).
   - Key Achievement: Visited Vesta (2011-2012) and Ceres (2015-2018). Confirmed Ceres is an active world with brine reservoirs, identifying sodium carbonate in Occator Crater.

4. NEW HORIZONS MISSION (NASA)
   - Operation: 2006 (Launch), Pluto flyby on July 14, 2015.
   - Key Achievement: Revealed Sputnik Planitia (an active nitrogen-ice glacier) and evidence of ice volcanoes (cryovolcanism) like Wright Mons on Pluto.

5. EUROPA CLIPPER (NASA)
   - Operation: Launched in October 2024; expected arrival in the Jupiter system in 2030.
   - Key Objective: Will perform multiple close flybys of Europa to analyze its habitability using the MISE (Mapping Imaging Spectrometer for Europa) instrument.

SECTION 6: UPCOMING AND FUTURE MISSIONS (NEW)
---
The next decade promises a new wave of exploration targeting the icy worlds of the outer Solar System.

1. DRAGONFLY (NASA)
   - Target: Titan (moon of Saturn)
   - Launch: Scheduled for 2028, arrival in 2034.
   - Mission Profile: A nuclear-powered rotorcraft lander that will hop across Titan's surface, covering hundreds of kilometers over its nominal 2.7-year mission. It will study prebiotic chemistry in Titan's organic-rich dune fields and crater lakes.
   - Science Goal: To determine whether the chemical building blocks of life exist or have existed on Titan's surface.

2. JUICE - JUPITER ICY MOONS EXPLORER (ESA)
   - Target: Ganymede (primary), Europa, and Callisto (flybys)
   - Launch: April 14, 2023 (successfully launched by ESA).
   - Arrival at Jupiter: July 2031.
   - Mission Profile: JUICE will become the first spacecraft to orbit a moon of an outer planet (Ganymede) starting in 2034. It carries 10 science instruments including a radar sounder (RIME) to penetrate beneath Ganymede's icy crust.
   - Connection to StudyMate Context: JUICE directly complements Europa Clipper; while Clipper focuses exclusively on Europa, JUICE will compare multiple icy moons within the same Jovian system.

3. ENCELADUS ORBILANDER (CONCEPT)
   - Status: A concept mission endorsed in NASA's Planetary Science Decadal Survey 2023-2032.
   - Mission Profile: A combined orbiter and lander that would sample Enceladus' south polar plumes directly and land on the surface to search for biosignatures. It is considered the highest-priority large mission concept for the decade starting 2033.

SECTION 7: THE CHEMISTRY OF LIFE - WHAT MAKES AN OCEAN HABITABLE? (NEW)
---
Astrobiologists define habitability through a framework of interconnected requirements. The presence of liquid water alone is not sufficient; several additional chemical and energy conditions must be satisfied.

The CHNOPS Framework:
Life as we know it is built from six primary elements: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Nitrogen (N), Oxygen (O), Phosphorus (P), and Sulfur (S). The detection of any of these elements in a subsurface ocean is a positive habitability indicator.

Energy Sources:
Two primary metabolic energy strategies are relevant to icy moon environments:
1. Chemolithotrophy: Organisms derive energy from inorganic chemical reactions, such as the oxidation of hydrogen gas (H2) in hydrothermal vent systems. The detection of H2 by Cassini in Enceladus' plumes is a direct biosignature of potential serpentinization, providing a viable energy source.
2. Radiolysis: High-energy particles (cosmic rays and Jupiter's radiation belt) can break apart water ice molecules on Europa's surface, generating oxidants (such as hydrogen peroxide, H2O2) that could be delivered to the subsurface ocean through ice shell mixing, providing an oxidant supply for potential life.

The pH and Salinity Problem:
Ocean chemistry can also restrict habitability. Analysis of Enceladus' plume particles by Cassini indicated a mildly alkaline ocean with a pH of approximately 11 to 12. This is within the range tolerated by certain extremophile organisms on Earth, such as those found in soda lakes. However, the saline composition and specific ion concentrations remain active research questions.

SECTION 8: TRANS-NEPTUNIAN OBJECTS - BEYOND THE FIVE RECOGNIZED DWARF PLANETS (NEW)
---
The IAU currently recognizes only five dwarf planets, but the trans-Neptunian region likely contains hundreds of additional bodies meeting the size threshold for hydrostatic equilibrium. Several are of particular scientific interest.

1. SEDNA
   - Discovery Year: 2003 (by Mike Brown, Chad Trujillo, and David Rabinowitz)
   - Physical Profile: Estimated diameter of 900 to 1,000 kilometers. Its surface is one of the reddest objects in the Solar System.
   - Orbit Period: Approximately 11,400 Earth years -- one of the longest known orbital periods of any Solar System object.
   - Significance: Sedna's orbit is so elongated (perihelion at 76 AU, aphelion at ~936 AU) that it may be influenced by an undiscovered outer Solar System planet, sometimes referred to as "Planet Nine" or "Planet X." It resides in what some astronomers define as the Inner Oort Cloud.

2. QUAOAR
   - Discovery Year: 2002 (by Mike Brown and Chad Trujillo)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter of approximately 1,110 kilometers, making it a strong candidate for eventual IAU dwarf planet recognition.
   - Notable Discovery (2023): A dense ring system was confirmed around Quaoar, orbiting at a radius far beyond the Roche limit -- the zone within which tidal forces would normally prevent ring material from coalescing into a moon. This challenges existing ring formation models and remains an open research question in planetary science.

3. GONGGONG
   - Discovery Year: 2007 (by Megan Schwamb, Mike Brown, and David Rabinowitz)
   - Physical Profile: Diameter of approximately 1,230 kilometers.
   - Orbit: A highly eccentric orbit with an inclination of 30.7 degrees and an orbital period of ~554 Earth years.
   - Moon: Gonggong has one known moon, Xiangliu, discovered in 2016 using the Hubble Space Telescope.
